---
title: "A decision journal that grades your judgment, not your outcomes"
summary: "A product concept: log a decision and your reasoning before you know the result, then let the tool score your calibration over time — separate from whether you got lucky."
date: "2026-03-02"
status: "Sketch"
tags: ["Product concept", "Decision-making", "Personal tools"]
---

The idea: most of us learn the wrong lesson from our decisions, because we only evaluate them after we know the outcome, and outcomes are contaminated by luck. A good decision made under uncertainty can produce a bad result, and a bad decision can get bailed out by chance — but we remember the result, not the quality of the reasoning that produced it.

**The mechanic:** log a decision at the moment you make it — what you're choosing, your confidence level, and the reasoning — before the outcome is known. Weeks or months later, when the outcome resolves, the tool asks you to score the *outcome* separately from the *decision quality* you logged at the time. Over enough entries, it can show you your calibration: are your 80%-confidence bets right about 80% of the time? Are you systematically overconfident in one category of decision (hiring, prioritization, timing) and underconfident in another?

**Why it's still a sketch, not a product:** the honest problem is retention. A tool like this is valuable in aggregate, over dozens of entries, but each individual entry has almost no immediate payoff — you write it and get nothing back until much later. That's a hard shape for a habit to survive on. It probably needs to piggyback on something people already log regularly (a work journal, a 1:1 notes tool) rather than ask for a new habit outright.

**Open questions:**
- Does forcing a numeric confidence score improve calibration, or just teach people to game the score?
- Is the right unit of analysis the individual decision, or a *category* of decision — and how much of that categorization can be automatic versus manual tagging?
- Would this be more useful as a personal tool or a team one? Team calibration on shared decisions might be the more interesting — and more defensible — version.
